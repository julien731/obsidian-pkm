Research conducted 2026-04-01 on the #buildinpublic movement on X/Twitter for solo founders, with focus on what works for a personal finance app launching from zero.

## State of the Movement (2025-2026)

The movement is mature but still effective — with a higher quality bar. Low-effort "Day 14 of building my SaaS" posts get ignored. The audience is more skeptical: they know build-in-public is a strategy, so authenticity matters even more.

**Key dynamics:**
- Fatigue with low-effort posts is real — the bar has risen significantly
- Some builders have migrated to Bluesky, Threads, or newsletters, but X remains the largest hub
- The indie hacker community is segmented: finance/productivity, AI, dev tools each have sub-communities
- What still gets genuine engagement: real numbers, honest failures, useful insights, and personality

## What Works Now

### High-performing content formats

1. **Concrete numbers** — revenue screenshots, user counts, conversion rates. "My budgeting app hit $1K MRR after 6 months" outperforms vague updates every time.
2. **Lessons from failure** — "I spent 3 weeks building X feature and nobody used it. Here's what I learned" gets far more engagement than success posts.
3. **Before/after screenshots** — UI evolution, landing page redesigns, dashboard improvements. Visual content massively outperforms text-only.
4. **Short threads (3-5 tweets)** revealing a specific decision, tradeoff, or insight. Long 20-tweet threads have declining engagement.
5. **Hot takes with substance** — contrarian opinions about tools, frameworks, or business strategy backed by personal experience.
6. **"How I built X" technical breakdowns** — especially if they save others time.

### What no longer works

- Generic daily updates with no insight ("Worked on auth today")
- Revenue screenshots without context or lessons
- Engagement-bait questions ("What's your tech stack?")
- Pure self-promotion disguised as building in public

## Successful Examples

### Pieter Levels (@levelsio)

The godfather. Built NomadList, RemoteOK, PhotoAI. Key formula: extreme transparency about revenue (displays it publicly), ships fast (often in days), provocative opinions, builds tools people can immediately see/use.

### Marc Lou (@marc_louvion)

Multiple micro-SaaS products. Grew rapidly sharing exact revenue numbers, offering a "ship in 5 days" narrative. Turned the build-in-public process itself into a product (ShipFast boilerplate).

### Danny Postma (@dannypostma)

Headshot Pro, ProfilePicture.ai. Shared specific growth metrics and was transparent about which marketing channels worked.

### Jen Yip (@laborovulay) / Lunch Money

**Most relevant for a budgeting app.** Solo developer building a personal finance/budgeting app.
- Transparent about being solo and building a finance tool
- Shared technical decisions, revenue milestones, and emotional journey
- Content resonated because personal finance is relatable
- Grew steadily rather than virally — took months of consistent posting
- Balanced technical content with product/UX content
- Reached profitability solo, which became part of the narrative

### Jon Yongfook (@yongfook) / Bannerbear

Monthly revenue updates with honest commentary. "Open startup" approach (public revenue dashboard) drove credibility. Took well over a year of consistent building and posting before significant traction.

### Plausible Analytics (@PlausibleHQ)

Values-driven build-in-public: positioning against Google Analytics, sharing why privacy matters. Key lesson: **having a clear "enemy" gave their narrative a compelling arc.**

|---|---|
| 0-3 months | 100-500 followers, mostly other indie hackers |
| 3-6 months | 500-2,000 followers if posting consistently with substance |

Most successful builders took 6-18 months of consistent effort.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **All build, no insight.** "Pushed 12 commits today" teaches nothing. Every post should contain a transferable lesson or genuine transparency.
2. **Performative vulnerability.** Fake "I almost quit today" posts. The audience smells inauthenticity.
3. **Ignoring actual users to perform for X.** Build-in-public should serve the product, not replace product work.
4. **Revenue bragging without context.** "$10K MRR!" means nothing without margins, time invested, costs.
5. **Only talking to other indie hackers.** The #buildinpublic echo chamber is real. Actual customers probably aren't in it.
6. **Inconsistency.** Posting intensely for 2 weeks then going silent for a month.
7. **Not engaging with others.** The builders who grow fastest spend significant time replying, adding value in comments, and building relationships.
8. **Premature optimization.** Obsessing over best posting times with 50 followers.

## Tactical Playbook

### Posting

- **Frequency:** 1-2 substantive posts per day. Quality over quantity.
- **Format:** Single posts with an image/screenshot tend to outperform threads now. Threads only for genuinely compelling stories.
- **Hashtags:** #buildinpublic is primary. #indiehackers secondary. One per post max. Some successful builders have dropped hashtags entirely (they attract builders, not customers).
- **Timing:** US morning (8-10am ET) for global indie hacker audience. Consistency matters more than timing at low follower counts.

### Getting initial engagement with 0 followers

1. Reply meaningfully to larger accounts in your space — not "great post!" but adding genuine insight
2. Quote-tweet interesting posts with your own take
3. Join communities outside X (IndieHackers.com, Discord/Slack groups, Hacker News) and cross-pollinate
4. Post your first meaningful milestone (first user, first dollar, first feature) — these get community support
5. Tag relevant people when genuinely appropriate (not spam)

## Why Build-in-Public Works for a Finance App

Trust is paramount. People share sensitive financial data with budgeting apps. Build-in-public builds trust that a faceless app cannot:
- Showing your face and decision-making process
- Transparency about data privacy/security practices
- Using your own app publicly (dogfooding narrative)

## Key Takeaway

Build in public works but requires top-quartile quality. The advantage for a personal finance app: budgeting content has a much larger audience than #buildinpublic alone. By mixing product updates with genuine budgeting insights, you can reach actual potential users — not just other builders.
