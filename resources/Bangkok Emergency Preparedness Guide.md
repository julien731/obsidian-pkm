# Bangkok Emergency Preparedness Guide

Practical, Bangkok-specific emergency preparedness guide for a French expat living in Bangkok with a golden retriever, with an evacuation fallback toward the Pranburi/Hua Hin area (~200km south).

## 1. Bangkok Natural Disaster Risks

### Earthquakes

Bangkok sits on soft marine clay, which amplifies long-period seismic waves from distant earthquakes. The city has no major fault lines underneath it, but it is highly vulnerable to powerful quakes originating elsewhere.

**The 2025 precedent:** On March 28, 2025, a magnitude 7.7 earthquake struck central Myanmar (~1,000km from Bangkok). Despite the distance, Bangkok was severely affected:
- Tremors felt in 63 of Thailand's 77 provinces
- A skyscraper under construction collapsed, causing significant casualties (103 deaths in Thailand total)
- BTS Skytrain shut down, Stock Exchange suspended trading, malls closed
- Bangkok was officially declared a disaster area
- Traffic gridlocked as people evacuated buildings simultaneously
- Misinformation spread rapidly on social media

**Key takeaway:** Bangkok's geology means even distant earthquakes (Myanmar, Sumatra) can cause real damage, especially to high-rise buildings. If you live or work in a tall building, this is a genuine risk.

### Flooding

Flooding is Bangkok's most significant and recurring natural disaster risk. The city sits barely 1.5 meters above sea level on average and is sinking 1-2cm per year.

**The 2011 catastrophe:**
- Monsoon flooding submerged large parts of Bangkok for weeks to months (some districts flooded for 4 months)
- 815 deaths, 13.6 million people affected
- $46.5 billion in total damage (12.6% of GDP)
- Government declared a 5-day holiday to encourage people to leave Bangkok
- Public transport collapsed: buses stopped, taxis disappeared from affected areas
- Evacuation was chaotic with only ~24 hours warning via sirens/loudspeakers
- 2 hours to travel 5km by motorcycle through flooded streets
- Shops ran out of supplies and closed; price gouging on essentials

**Future outlook:** The World Bank predicts 40% of Bangkok could be inundated by 2030 due to extreme rainfall, land subsidence, and sea level rise. A 2011-scale flood is not a matter of "if" but "when."

**Flood season:** May through October (monsoon), with peak risk September-November. But the Chao Phraya river system means flooding can come from upstream provinces weeks after heavy rains there.

### Severe Storms

Thailand experiences tropical storms and occasional typhoon remnants, particularly during monsoon season. Southern Thailand is more directly affected, but Bangkok gets severe thunderstorms, flash flooding, and occasional storm damage. In November 2025, southern Thailand floods killed 33 people.

## 2. Political Instability

Thailand has experienced 13 successful coups since 1932. The most recent significant events:

**2014 Coup (most recent):**
- Nationwide curfew imposed: 22:00-05:00
- Media censorship, internet restrictions
- Hundreds of websites blocked immediately
- Gatherings of more than 5 people banned
- International flights continued operating
- Embassies advised nationals to stay indoors during curfew, avoid protest areas
- Foreign governments advised against non-essential travel

**2010 Military Crackdown:**
- 85+ killed including 2 foreigners
- Central Bangkok (Ratchaprasong/Silom area) was the conflict zone
- Arson attacks on Central World and other buildings

**2020-2021 Protests:**
- Large-scale youth-led protests across Bangkok
- Less violent but caused periodic disruption
- Occasional emergency decrees

**What practically happens to expats:**
- Curfews are enforced; violating them risks arrest
- International airports typically remain open (important for evacuation)
- Embassies issue shelter-in-place advisories
- Banks and ATMs generally continue operating
- Grocery stores may close early or temporarily
- Internet is restricted but not fully shut down (VPN essential)
- Protests concentrate in specific areas (Ratchaprasong, Democracy Monument, Government House) - these are avoidable

**Internet during political events:** After the 2014 coup, hundreds of websites were blocked in May 2014 alone. Thailand has over 110,000 blocked websites. Full internet shutdowns have not occurred, but heavy censorship and selective blocking are standard. Always have a VPN installed and configured.

## 3. Evacuation Routes: Bangkok to Pranburi/Hua Hin

### Primary Route: Rama II Road (Highway 35) to Highway 4

The most direct route south from Bangkok to Hua Hin/Pranburi.

- **Route:** Bangkok (Chom Thong) → Highway 35 (Rama II Road, 84km) → joins Highway 4 (Phetkasem Road) in Ratchaburi → continues south to Hua Hin/Pranburi
- **Normal drive time:** ~3 hours
- **Warning:** Rama II Road is one of Thailand's most dangerous highways. Between 2018-2024: 2,245 accidents, 136 deaths, 1,320 injuries. Perpetual construction, frequent accidents, nighttime lane closures (19:00-05:30 between Mahachai and Ban Phaeo).

### Alternative Route: Highway 338 to Highway 4 (avoiding Rama II)

- **Route:** Bangkok → Borommaratchachonnani Road (Highway 338) → Highway 4 (Phetkasem Road) through Nakhon Pathom and Ratchaburi → rejoin southern route at Wang Manao Intersection → south to Hua Hin/Pranburi
- **Distance:** ~125km longer than Rama II route
- **Advantage:** Avoids the most dangerous segment entirely

### Return/alternative routes via Samut Songkhram

- Highway 375 → Ban Phaeo → Nakhon Pathom → Phetkasem Road → Borommaratchachonnani Road
- From Mahachai/Samut Sakhon: Setthakit Road → Phutthasakhon Road → Phutthamonthon Sai 4 → Borommaratchachonnani Road

### Road conditions during disasters

- During the 2011 floods, roads south of Bangkok were partially flooded in some areas
- During the 2025 earthquake, Bangkok traffic completely gridlocked as everyone tried to leave simultaneously
- Highway 35 (Rama II) has had structural collapses on its elevated sections (most recently in 2025, killing 5)
- **Keep fuel above half tank at all times.** Gas stations may close or run dry during emergencies.

**Department of Highways hotline:** 1586 (toll-free, 24/7) for real-time road conditions.

## 5. Emergency Supplies - Bangkok-Specific

### What runs out first (based on 2011 floods and 2025 earthquake)

1. **Bottled drinking water** - First thing to disappear from shelves. Cheap brands from 7-Eleven, Family Mart, Big C, and Tesco Lotus emptied first.
2. **Instant noodles** - Second to go
3. **Sandbags** - Sold out and price-gouged during flood warnings
4. **Rubber boots/wading gear** - Unavailable during floods
5. **Fresh food** - Supply chains break down within days
6. **Batteries and power banks** - High demand during power outages
7. **Fuel** - Gas stations close or run dry

### Recommended permanent stockpile

- **Water:** Minimum 20 liters per person (more for the dog). Replace every 6 months. Consider a gravity water filter (Berkey or similar) that can purify tap water or rainwater.
- **Food:** 2 weeks of non-perishable food. Rice (keeps well in airtight containers in Bangkok's humidity), canned goods, instant noodles, dried fruit, nuts.
- **Dog food:** 2 weeks of kibble in airtight sealed containers (humidity degrades open bags fast). Canned wet food as backup.
- **Medications:** 30-day supply of any regular medications. Basic antibiotics if obtainable. Anti-diarrheal (critical during floods when water quality drops).
- **Cash:** See financial preparedness section below.
- **Fuel:** Keep car above half tank at all times. Consider storing 10-20 liters of fuel safely (check condo/house regulations).

## 6. Pet Evacuation - Golden Retriever

### Documents to keep ready at all times

Keep a waterproof folder with:
- **Rabies vaccination certificate** (must include microchip number, inoculation date, validity period) - must be current
- **DHLPP vaccination records** (Distemper, Hepatitis, Leptospirosis, Parvovirus, Parainfluenza) - must be between 21 days and 1 year old
- **Microchip documentation**
- **Pet passport photos** (if available)
- **Veterinarian contact information**

### Domestic travel (within Thailand)

Moving within Thailand with a dog is relatively straightforward - no permits needed for road travel. The main challenge is logistics: a golden retriever needs space in the vehicle, water, and cooling (critical in Bangkok heat).

### International border crossing (if needed)

If evacuation requires leaving Thailand:
- **Thailand export permit** required from the Bangkok Animal Quarantine Station (takes days to obtain in normal circumstances)
- **Health certificate** - can sometimes be issued at land border crossings if you have proof of all vaccinations and microchip
- **Destination country import permit** - varies by country
- **Rabies titer test** may be required for some destinations

**Practical reality:** In a true emergency requiring you to leave Thailand quickly by land, pet paperwork becomes a significant obstacle. Pre-prepare as much documentation as possible. The Cambodian and Malaysian borders have different requirements. Flying out with a large dog requires advance booking with airlines.

### Go-bag for the dog

- 1 week of kibble in sealed container
- Collapsible water bowl
- 10 liters of water specifically for the dog
- Leash, harness, muzzle (required in some situations)
- Any medications
- Recent photo of you with the dog (proof of ownership)
- Familiar blanket or toy (reduces stress)
- Paw protection (Bangkok streets flood with contaminated water)

## 7. Cash and Financial Preparedness

### ATM limits in Thailand

- **Standard withdrawal:** 20,000-30,000 THB per transaction
- **Krungsri (yellow ATMs):** 30,000 THB max per transaction
- **Krungthai Bank:** 25,000 THB max per transaction
- **Citibank:** 50,000 THB max per transaction (best for large withdrawals)
- **Aeon ATMs:** Lowest foreign card fee at 150 THB (vs standard 250 THB)
- **No daily transaction limit** on most Thai ATMs (but your home bank may impose one)

### During emergencies

- ATMs may run out of cash or lose power
- Bank branches may close
- Electronic payments may fail if internet/power is disrupted
- Credit cards may not work at small shops

### Recommended cash reserve

Keep **50,000-100,000 THB in cash** at home in a waterproof container, in mixed denominations:
- 1,000 THB notes for larger purchases
- 100 and 500 THB notes for everyday transactions
- 20 and 50 THB notes for small purchases when change is unavailable

Additionally:
- Keep **500-1,000 USD in cash** as universal emergency currency
- **Western Union** is available throughout Thailand as emergency backup (only need passport to collect)
- Consider maintaining accounts at 2 different Thai banks for redundancy
- Keep some cash in your go-bag, separate from your home stash

## 8. Go-Bag for Tropical Climate

A tropical go-bag differs significantly from temperate climate kits.

### Unique tropical considerations

- **Heat and humidity degrade supplies faster:** Rotate food, batteries, and medications more frequently (every 6 months)
- **Infection risk is higher:** Small cuts become infected quickly in Bangkok's heat. Antiseptic is critical.
- **Dehydration is the primary health threat:** More water and electrolytes than a temperate kit
- **Mold and moisture damage:** Everything must be in waterproof bags/containers
- **Insects carry disease:** Dengue and malaria mosquitoes breed in standing floodwater

### Go-bag contents (per person)

**Documents (waterproof pouch):**
- Passport (or certified copy)
- Thai work permit / visa documentation
- Insurance documents
- Embassy contact numbers
- Cash reserve (see above)
- Pet documentation
- USB drive with scanned copies of all important documents

**Water and food:**
- 3 liters of water minimum (more for summer months)
- Electrolyte powder packets (10+)
- Energy bars, dried fruit, nuts
- Water purification tablets

**Clothing:**
- Lightweight, moisture-wicking clothes (cotton/linen, not synthetic)
- Rain poncho (takes minimal space, essential)
- Dry bag for keeping items dry during flooding/rain

**Health:**
- Antiseptic wipes and spray (wound infection is the #1 tropical health risk)
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics if obtainable
- Anti-diarrheal medication (Imodium)
- Oral rehydration salts
- DEET or Picaridin insect repellent
- Sunscreen SPF 30+
- Antihistamines
- Personal medications (30-day supply)
- N95 masks (air quality in Bangkok is frequently hazardous)

**Electronics:**
- Power bank (20,000+ mAh), fully charged
- Phone charging cables
- Battery-powered or hand-crank radio
- Flashlight with extra batteries
- Waterproof phone case

**Tools:**
- Multi-tool or Swiss army knife
- Duct tape (small roll)
- Whistle
- Microfiber towel (dries fast in humidity)

## 9. Communication During Emergencies

### Mobile networks

- **AIS** - Best overall coverage, especially outside Bangkok and in rural/border areas. Score of 8.5/10 for coverage experience. **Recommended as primary SIM.**
- **DTAC** - Best reliability score (883/1000) within coverage areas
- **TrueMove H** - Adequate in Bangkok, weaker in rural areas

**Recommendation:** Carry two SIMs (AIS + DTAC/True) for redundancy. If you only have one, make it AIS.

### Internet during political events

Thailand has never done a full internet blackout, but:
- Hundreds of websites blocked during the 2014 coup
- Social media platforms throttled or blocked during protests
- VPN services sometimes targeted but generally still work
- 110,000+ websites permanently blocked in Thailand

**Essential preparations:**
- Install and configure a VPN before any crisis (ExpressVPN and NordVPN work well in Thailand)
- Download offline maps (Google Maps allows offline areas, or use Maps.me/OsmAnd)
- Have the Ariane app installed for French embassy alerts
- Save embassy phone numbers in your phone
- Consider a satellite communicator (Garmin inReach or similar) for true communication independence

### Emergency numbers

- **Police:** 191
- **Tourist Police:** 1155 (English-speaking)
- **Fire:** 199
- **Ambulance:** 1669
- **Department of Disaster Prevention:** 1784
- **Department of Highways:** 1586
- **French Embassy emergency:** +66 81 994 4901

### Alternative communication

- **LINE app** is Thailand's dominant messaging platform (more reliable than WhatsApp during Thai internet disruptions as it's hosted on Asian servers)
- **FM Radio** - keep a battery-powered radio; Thai PBS and FM91 provide English-language emergency broadcasts
- **Ham radio** - if you're serious about preparedness, amateur radio requires no infrastructure

## 10. Regional Conflict Risks

### South China Sea

- Thailand is not a claimant state in the South China Sea dispute
- Thailand maintains careful neutrality between the US and China
- Direct military conflict affecting Bangkok is extremely unlikely
- **Realistic impact:** Disruption to shipping lanes could cause supply chain issues and fuel price increases. If conflict escalates between China and the Philippines/US, Thailand would likely try to stay neutral but could face economic pressure from both sides.

### Myanmar Instability

- Myanmar shares a long border with Thailand (western and northern borders)
- Ongoing civil war since the 2021 coup continues with no resolution in sight
- Myanmar's military junta signaling phased elections starting January 2026, widely seen as illegitimate
- **Realistic impact on Bangkok:**
  - Refugee flows into Thailand (already happening at scale)
  - Cross-border weapons and drug trafficking
  - The 2025 earthquake demonstrated that natural disasters in Myanmar directly affect Bangkok
  - If Chinese weapons flow into Myanmar to support the junta, prolonged civil war and increased refugee pressure on Thailand

### Thailand-Cambodia Border Tensions

This is a more immediate concern. Heavy fighting occurred twice in 2025:
- July 2025 border clash
- December 2025: nearly three weeks of fighting
- A ceasefire is in place but the relationship remains fragile
- In June 2025, Cambodia cut all internet bandwidth from Thailand during tensions
- **Realistic impact on Bangkok:** Minimal direct risk, but complicates any evacuation plan that involves heading east. The southern route toward Hua Hin/Pranburi is unaffected.

### Thailand Domestic Politics

Thailand held a general election on February 8, 2026. Civil-military relations and constitutional reform remain contentious. The risk of another political crisis or coup, while lower than in previous decades, is never zero in Thailand.

### Middle East Airspace Crisis (2026)

As of early 2026, Middle East airspace disruptions have stranded ~50,000 travelers in Thailand, including thousands of French nationals. Thailand's Immigration Bureau granted automatic visa grace periods. This is a reminder that **air evacuation routes can be cut off by distant conflicts** - always have a land-based evacuation plan.
