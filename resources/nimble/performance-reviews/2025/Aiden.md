# 360 Review: Aiden (2025)

## Summary

**Strengths:**
- High ownership and proactiveness (4 reviewers) — takes charge, follows up on progress, steps in to help even outside her direct responsibilities
- Strong work ethic and capacity (3) — consistently shows up, stays engaged even on leave, handles high workload under pressure
- Clear communication of priorities (3) — keeps the team aligned on direction and what problems they're solving
- UX advocacy and design engagement (2) — actively participates in design reviews, identifies blockers early, pushes for UI/UX improvements

**Areas for improvement:**
- Written communication tone (3) — text messages can come across as blunt or blaming; different from her tone on calls. Choice of words matters
- Cross-platform context and documentation (2) — decisions and constraints not always captured in tickets, leading to back-and-forth and recaps
- Upfront requirement validation (2) — assumptions and constraints should be clarified before design starts to avoid rework cycles
- Pressure management (1) — a more measured approach in challenging moments would ease pressure on the dev team
- Consistency in decision-making (1) — sometimes doesn't fight for her ideas, other times fights for the wrong ones; needs more consistency

### From Beckham

Given the current project’s complexity spanning both mobile and web platforms, Aiden is manage and cross checking if the solution work for both the platforms.

Occasionally, Web team members need to spend time recapping ticket context or clarifying details before getting answers, which suggests it can be hard to keep track of every thread.

One thing that could help is establishing a lightweight habit of documenting platform specific decisions directly in tickets, for example (Something like we have for JB region support for VN), explicitly noting whether a solution applies to mobile, web, or both. This would reduce the need for back-and-forth clarification and make it easier for Aiden to context switch quickly without losing detail. It would also benefit the broader team by making decisions more visible and reducing reliance on verbal recaps.

### From Thien

In JB Loyalty Project, there was some initial misalignment with client requirements, which led to multiple rounds of design revisions and back-and-forth adjustments. While the team ultimately delivered successfully, the rework created additional workload and timeline pressure.

I believe there is an opportunity to strengthen upfront requirement validation — for example, by documenting assumptions more explicitly, confirming edge cases early, or conducting structured walkthroughs with the client before moving into design. Establishing clearer alignment at the beginning could help reduce iteration cycles, protect team bandwidth, and increase delivery predictability.
