# 360 Review: Jack (2025)

## Summary

**Strengths:**
- Proactive and always ahead (4 reviewers) — anticipates needs, prepares documentation, reaches out to clients for clarification before being asked
- Clear communication and task breakdown (4) — translates complex information into clear, developer-friendly stories with proper context
- Strong client management (3) — balances expectations with team capacity, pushes back professionally on unclear or unrealistic requests
- Calm under pressure (2) — makes urgent situations feel manageable, maintains composure
- Reliable and organized (2) — never forgets things, keeps projects under control

**Areas for improvement:**
- Sprint readiness with external dependencies (1) — tickets sometimes pending client decisions after sprint start; preparing them earlier would help
- Earlier QA on tickets (1) — doing QA sooner on "Ready for QA" tickets would catch issues earlier and reduce release builds
- Frustration management (1) — the shared complaint list about a client PM was counterproductive; needs better channels for frustrations
- Alignment with stakeholders (1) — could communicate more with certain stakeholders to reduce feature misalignment

Everyone independently described Jack as being "ahead" or "a step ahead." This isn't generic praise — each reviewer tied it to concrete behaviors: preparing documentation before being asked (Doan), chasing up the client preemptively (James), clarifying requirements before sprint (Zaw). This is a genuine, observable pattern, not just a reputation.

**Clear communication and task breakdown (4 reviewers: Zaw, Htoo, Taus, Doan Le Thieu):**
Multiple reviewers specifically noted Jack's ability to translate complex information into something understandable for their role — Taus as a designer, Htoo and Doan as engineers. **Client management and pushback (3 reviewers: James, Doan Le Thieu, Zaw):**
Jack doesn't just relay client requests — he filters, challenges, and shapes them. Doan specifically noted he examines feasibility and intent before passing things to the team. James trusts him to have "the project under control." This is a high-trust PM skill.

**Composure under pressure (2 reviewers: Taus, James):**
Taus called calmness a "standout personality" trait. James described consistent reliability. This came through even in a short engagement (Taus worked with Jack briefly on Smashburger).

### Diverging Perspectives

- **Frustration management:** James flagged the Notion complaint list as a real concern — it turned into an "attack" on the JFC PM. No other reviewer mentioned this, but James's account is specific and credible. Julien's observation about resilience suggests Jack has improved since, but this is worth addressing directly.
- **Communication gaps:** Htoo says Jack needs to talk more with Carlos to reduce feature misalignment. Taus says she sometimes lacked context for urgent tasks. These point to a pattern: Jack's proactivity is strong with clients but may have gaps with certain internal stakeholders — particularly when urgency compresses the usual process.

### Blind Spots

- **Over-engineering requirements:** Julien's feedback about "too much focus on edge cases" and "what if" scenarios is something no peer reviewer would typically see — it's a strategic-level concern about how Jack defines scope. The Okya user story issue (technical details that shouldn't have been there) suggests Jack sometimes goes deep into territory that isn't his to define, which is the flip side of his thoroughness.
- **Pragmatism vs. thoroughness trade-off:** Jack's strength (anticipating everything, preparing documentation) has a shadow side — he may over-prepare and over-scope when a leaner MVP approach would serve the project better. No reviewer flagged this because from their perspective, more preparation is always welcome. Only a manager looking at project-level efficiency would see the cost.
- **Hands-on gap:** Julien notes Jack experiments with AI but hasn't yet applied it to his actual PM work. Given the industry shift, this is a significant growth area that peers wouldn't flag.

### Strength-to-Growth-Area Connections

| Strength | Connected Growth Area |
|----------|----------------------|
| Thorough preparation and anticipation | Over-scoping MVPs, spending time on edge cases that may never matter |
| Strong client pushback | Frustration with difficult client stakeholders can spill into counterproductive venting (the Notion list incident) |
| Deep technical understanding | Risk of overstepping into technical decisions that should stay with engineers (Okya user stories) |
| Proactive documentation | May not extend equally to all internal stakeholders (Htoo's Carlos point, Taus's lack of context) |

### Development Recommendations

1. **Apply the "what's the cheapest way to learn?" filter to MVP definitions.** Before adding a requirement or edge case to a story, ask: "If we skip this, what's the worst that happens and how quickly would we know?" Write this question on a sticky note. Jack's instinct is to cover everything — he needs a deliberate counterweight toward "ship and learn."

2. **Create a frustration protocol for difficult client relationships.** The Notion complaint list happened because there was no structured outlet. Recommendation: when a client relationship is generating friction, schedule a weekly 15-minute "vent and strategize" with Julien or Vu — time-boxed, private, and action-oriented. The goal is to channel frustration into strategy rather than shared grievance lists.

3. **Close the stakeholder communication gaps.** Specifically with Carlos (per Htoo) and with designers on urgent tasks (per Taus). Concrete action: for any ticket that changes scope or priority mid-sprint, send a 2-line Slack message to affected people with context and expected timeline — even if it feels obvious. Jack's proactivity with clients should extend to internal stakeholders with the same consistency.

4. **Start using AI tools in actual PM workflows within the next quarter.** Jack already experiments with AI for building things — now apply it to writing user stories, synthesizing client feedback, drafting sprint goals, or generating first-pass acceptance criteria. Pick one recurring PM task and commit to doing it with AI for 4 consecutive sprints. Report back on what worked.

5. **Draw a clearer line between "providing context" and "making technical decisions" in user stories.** The Okya incident suggests Jack sometimes crosses from helpful context into prescriptive technical detail. Rule of thumb: if a sentence in a user story would only make sense to an engineer, it probably belongs in a technical discussion, not the ticket.
