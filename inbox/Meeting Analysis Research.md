
Research compiled for refining meeting analysis templates and developing team performance analytics.

## Meeting Effectiveness Principles

### Meta-Principle
A meeting is effective when it changes what people will do next — not just what they think.

### Four Assessment Dimensions

**1. Decision yield vs. airtime**
- How many resolved decisions came out relative to total time spent?
- High-performing meetings front-load decisions and use remaining time to stress-test them, not discover them
- Low yield = lots of discussion, few concrete outcomes

**2. Data readiness**
- Were the right inputs available before the meeting started?
- Identify discussion that was structurally unresolvable because blocking information wasn't in the room
- Good meetings don't convene until the blocking input is available

**3. Circular discussion rate**
- How many times did the group revisit a point that had already reached a conclusion?
- Once is healthy (someone caught something)
- Twice suggests the conclusion wasn't clean
- Three+ times indicates a facilitation problem or unresolved interpersonal dynamic

**4. Intent vs. words**
- When someone raises a concern multiple times in different language, the stated concern probably isn't the real one
- When someone shuts down hypotheticals but keeps generating them, there's anxiety underneath worth surfacing
- Meetings that only operate at face value miss what's actually blocking progress

## Contribution Quality (Airtime ≠ Value)

### The Volume Trap
LLMs (and humans) tend to equate airtime with importance. Must distinguish:
- **Airtime** — How much someone spoke
- **Signal** — How much of what they said moved the conversation forward

Someone speaking loudly, frequently, or over others may actually be:
- Going in circles (repeating without new information)
- Asserting rather than persuading (stating positions without building cases)
- Filling silence rather than adding value
- Dominating without leading (controlling airtime without driving outcomes)

### What to Look For
- Who introduced ideas that were adopted?
- Who asked questions that unlocked stuck discussions?
- Who synthesized disparate points into actionable conclusions?
- Note: The most valuable contributor may have spoken very little

### The 40% Rule
No single speaker should consume more than 40% of talk-time in a healthy meeting.

## Psychological Safety Research

### Source
Amy Edmondson (Harvard) + Google Project Aristotle (180 teams, 2 years)

### Key Finding
Psychological safety is the #1 predictor of team performance — stronger than team composition, resources, or expertise.

### Warning Signs (Detectable in Transcripts)
- Silence from certain participants (especially junior with relevant expertise)
- Information hoarding
- Scapegoating
- Reluctance to raise concerns
- Hedging when disagreeing with senior people
- Questions not asked

### Important Note
True psychological safety assessment requires patterns across multiple meetings. Single-meeting observations are indicators, not diagnoses.

## Hidden Profiles / Shared Information Bias

### The Problem
Groups spend more time discussing information everyone already knows vs. unique information held by individuals.

### Research Findings
- Groups with prediscussion dissent (even incorrect dissent) have significantly higher solution rates
- When members express preferences at the start, groups are less likely to uncover hidden information
- Anonymous input surfaces 130-190 opinions vs. 50-90 in normal discussion — **half of valuable thinking stays hidden**

### Detection Signals
- Rapid agreement without expressed dissent
- Early preference anchoring ("I think we should...")
- Unique perspectives going unexplored
- Convergence before alternatives discussed

## Question Quality Research

### Source
Gong analysis of 326,000 sales calls

### Key Findings
- Winners ask 15-16 questions; losers ask 20 (interrogation-style backfires)
- **Interactivity** (back-and-forth exchanges) predicts success better than talk ratio
- Optimal monologue length: under 76-180 seconds
- Question quality matters more than quantity

### Question Types to Distinguish
- Clarifying questions (seeking understanding)
- Challenging questions (testing assumptions)
- Leading questions (steering toward predetermined answer)
- Open vs. closed questions

## Hedging Language as Signal

### What It Is
Words like "might," "could," "perhaps," "sort of," "kind of," "I think," "it seems"

### What It Indicates
- Uncertainty
- Lack of commitment
- Political positioning
- Deferred disagreement
- Fear of direct challenge

### Types
- **Approximators**: somewhat, more or less, roughly
- **Shields**: I think, it seems, in my opinion

### Actionable Insight
High hedging frequency from specific individuals, especially when disagreeing upward, may indicate lack of psychological safety or genuine uncertainty worth exploring.

## Network Roles in Meetings

### Three Leadership Functions
1. **Central actor** — Dominates the conversation flow
2. **Broker** — Connects different people/ideas
3. **Elicitor** — Draws out others' contributions

### Key Finding
The **elicitor role** correlates most strongly with participant satisfaction and team productivity. Group-level centralization (one person dominating connections) has **negative** effect on team performance.

### What to Track
- Who speaks to whom (response patterns)
- Is information flowing freely or through a central node?
- Who brings in quiet participants?

## Groupthink Detection

### Janis's Symptoms
- Illusions of invulnerability
- Stereotyping dissenters
- Pressure to conform
- Self-appointed "mindguards" who suppress dissent

### Research Data
- Asch conformity: 75% of participants conform at least once to clearly incorrect majority opinion
- Fatigued individuals show increased conformity in virtual meetings
- Factors amplifying: Senior status signaling "acceptable" views, rushed agendas, remote meetings creating "digital echo chambers"

### Convergence Speed as Warning
Rapid agreement without dissent suggests:
- Groupthink
- Hidden profile situation (unique information not surfaced)
- Psychological safety issues

## Action Item Completion Problem

### The Data
- **44% of meeting action items are never completed**
- 71% of meetings fail to achieve stated objectives due to poor follow-through

### Why Follow-through Fails
- Documenting "we discussed X" instead of "we decided Y"
- Shared ownership = no ownership (diffusion of responsibility)
- Missing deadlines, missing owners, vague action items

### Commitment Strength Spectrum
Strong: "I will deliver X by Friday"
Weak: "We should explore this"
Vague: "Someone needs to look into that"

## Topic Drift Detection

### Markers
- "anyway"
- "speaking of X"
- "so"
- "oh"

### Types
- **Local drift**: New topic within same thread
- **Global drift**: Outside meeting goals entirely

### Finding
Original speakers (who introduced the topic) put in most effort to bring conversations back on track.

## Meeting Cost Metric

### Formula
Cost = duration × participant count × estimated hourly rate

### Context
- ~$25,000/employee/year spent on meeting time
- Enables "cost per decision" calculation
- Quantifies whether meetings exceed their expense

## ROTI (Return on Time Invested)

### Source
Esther Derby

### Method
Simple 0-5 rating asking if time was worthwhile. Anyone rating 3 or lower shares what would have increased their rating by one point.

### Results
2023 study: Businesses tracking ROTI reduced wasted time by 28%, increased productivity by 18% in six months.

## Silence Analysis

### Two Types of Silence

**Productive silence:**
- Silent brainstorming produces more, higher-quality ideas than vocal sessions
- Bezos's "silent meetings": First 30 minutes for reading/reflection makes subsequent conversation more focused
- Neutralizes fear of peer evaluation

**Unproductive silence:**
- Junior associates with relevant expertise not contributing
- "The deeper the silence, the thicker the tension"
- Undiscussed differences increase latent conflict probability

### Silence as Speech Act
Silence can be an implicit response — choosing not to speak IS a form of communication.

## Audio-Based Signals (Requires Audio Analysis)

### What Could Be Captured
- Emotional tone per speaker (frustration, excitement, uncertainty, confidence)
- Prosodic features (volume patterns, pace, pitch variation)
- Interruption frequency and direction
- Speaking over others vs. natural turn-taking
- Energy dynamics across the meeting

### Value
Would enable analysis like:
- "Speaker A's frustration increased each time Topic X was raised"
- "The verbal agreement at 32:15 was accompanied by a resigned tone — may not reflect genuine buy-in"
